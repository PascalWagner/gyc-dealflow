Risks

What could go wrong?

🔒 3-year commitment — and that’s by design

Trade-off▼
The 3-year commitment is what gives you access to these private credit returns. If these funds were liquid, they wouldn’t pay 10%+. The lockup is the premium — it’s the reason this yield exists. After 3 years, quarterly partial or full redemptions are available with 90 days’ notice.

📈 Interest rates could compress yields

Moderate▼
6 of our 10 funds (65% of allocation) have some sensitivity to interest rates. Our 3 rate-independent funds provide meaningful insulation.

⚠️ An underlying fund could fail

Unlikely▼
Every fund is audited, with multi-year track records. No single fund holds more than 20%. For principal to be at material risk, 3+ funds would need to fail simultaneously.

👥 You’re trusting Pascal to manage this

Operator risk▼
Full bank statement transparency, GP co-invests alongside LPs, open calendar for any LP to schedule a review.

❌ Returns depend on 10 fund managers performing

Reality▼
8–10% is a target, not a promise. Your income comes from 10 independent fund managers each running their own lending operations. If any of them underperform, it affects the blended yield. LP distributions are paid before GP carry, giving you first priority on income.

No guarantees. But 10 independent managers, all first-position, all audited, across 5 asset classes.

External factors that could affect fund performance
⚠ Interest rates
Affects 6 of 10 funds (65% of allocation)
Rising rates slow CRE refinancing cycles and can reduce origination volume for bridge lenders. Our 3 rate-independent funds (Stratus, PFD, Alcova) provide meaningful insulation.
⚠ Credit cycle / recession
Affects all 10 funds — but with wide variance
An economic downturn increases borrower defaults. Most likely outcome: yield compression (returns fall toward preferred return floor), not principal loss. All funds hold 1st position with significant equity cushion.
⚠ Real estate market
Direct impact on 6 RE-focused funds
A broad CRE decline would affect DLP, Dwight, Reliant, Fairbridge, BRC, and Kirkland. Mitigated by geographic diversification, short loan durations, and conservative LTVs.
✓ Airline industry (Stratus)
Unique exposure — airline hiring cycles
Stratus performance is driven by pilot demand. Airlines have a structural shortage of 50,000+ pilots through 2030. An AI-driven replacement of pilots would take a minimum of 10–15 years and multiple regulatory approvals — well outside Stratus loan duration.
✓ Healthcare / legal (PFD)
Driven by PI litigation volume
Personal injury case volumes are largely uncorrelated to macro economic cycles. Tort reform legislation is the key risk — a federal cap on PI damages would be needed, which has failed to pass for 30+ years.
✓ Regulatory / government (Dwight)
HUD program risk — very low probability
Dwight's loans are HUD-qualified bridge loans. Eliminating HUD would require an act of Congress. The program has existed since 1934 and provides government-backed housing finance.
Capital risk scenarios
What would need to go sideways for capital to be at risk?

Our capital sits in first-position, diversified across 10 independent fund managers. Here's what would realistically need to happen for an LP to lose principal:

Scenario 1 — severe
Multiple fund failures simultaneously
3+ of our 10 underlying funds would need to simultaneously default and fail to recover collateral — requiring both manager fraud AND collateral collapse across multiple asset classes at the same time. Historically unprecedented in a diversified 1st-lien portfolio.
Scenario 2 — moderate
Extended liquidity freeze + forced sale
If an LP needed to exit during a credit market freeze before the 3-year lockup and GYC was forced to liquidate positions at distressed prices. Our quarterly redemption window after lockup mitigates forced-sale risk.
Most likely outcome
Return compression — not loss of principal
In a recession or credit cycle downturn, yields on some underlying funds may compress from 12–16% toward 8–10%. LPs would still receive distributions, just at lower rates. Capital is expected to be returned — the question is the yield, not the principal.
Bottom line
We don't guarantee returns — no fund can. But our structure is designed to absorb market stress: 10 independent managers, all first-position, all audited, all with multi-year track records. For capital to be materially at risk, we'd need a systemic credit collapse across multiple asset classes simultaneously. We monitor this quarterly and will exit positions that show deteriorating fundamentals before problems compound.
